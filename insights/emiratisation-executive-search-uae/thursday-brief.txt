LINKEDIN POST DRAFT

The Emiratisation deadline passed on June 30. Most companies treated it as a headcount
exercise: hit 9 percent Emirati representation in skilled roles, avoid the fine, move on.

That's the wrong read. The real test is starting now.

A company can meet its quota entirely through graduate and analyst hires and still have
zero UAE nationals anywhere near a P&L, a board seat, or a succession plan. Regulators
are counting skilled roles. Boards should be asking a harder question: who is our next
Emirati CFO, COO, or country manager, and are we developing them or just filling a form.

The data on retention is the real warning sign. Roughly half of Emirati private sector
hires leave because there's no visible next step. Some firms hire 200 graduates a year
and keep fewer than 20 after twelve months. Compliance without a leadership pipeline is
an expensive way to stand still.

Emiratisation executive search is a different discipline entirely. It means mapping
which Emirati professionals already have the credibility and sector depth to move into
director or VP roles within 12 to 18 months, not five years, and structuring those roles
so people actually stay. Nafis salary support now makes senior Emirati talent genuinely
cost-competitive too, which changes the calculation for a lot of employers.

The firms ahead on this stopped treating it as HR compliance about two years ago. Everyone
else is still catching up.
